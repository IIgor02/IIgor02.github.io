Distributional Functionals in Fixed-Effects Panels: A Weighted-Average Quantile Approach

Econometrics Panel Data Quantile Methods

Develops weighted-average quantile regression for fixed-effects panels when the target is an integrated feature of the conditional distribution, such as expected shortfall, lower-tail welfare, inequality-sensitive welfare, or distributional spread.

Read abstract

This article develops weighted-average quantile regression (WAQR) for fixed-effects panels when the object of interest is not a mean or an isolated quantile, but an integrated feature of the conditional distribution. The method is designed for questions about expected shortfall, lower-tail welfare, inequality-sensitive welfare, and distributional spread, where conventional fixed-effects regressions and pointwise panel quantile regressions answer different, and often incomplete, questions. WAQR estimates a smoothed fixed-effects quantile process and maps it into an orthogonal transformed outcome whose within-regression coefficient targets the chosen distributional functional. Cross-fitting and leave-one-out nuisance evaluation eliminate first-order own-observation feedback, while split-panel jackknife corrections remove the remaining incidental-parameter bias. For the normalized smoothed fixed-effects quantile first step, the leading two-way bias has additive 1/T and 1/n components and is removed by a full-panel, time-split, and cross-section-split combination. The resulting estimator is √nT-asymptotically normal and supports cluster-robust inference without estimating conditional densities. Simulations verify the bias correction and show that naive integration of fixed-effects quantile slopes can target the wrong estimand. Applications to international welfare and U.S. industry-portfolio returns show economically meaningful differences between mean, lower-tail, expected-shortfall, and spread effects.

A Threshold Spatial Durbin Panel Model: Estimation, Bias Correction, and Inference

with Fabrício Carneiro Linhares

Spatial Econometrics Panel Data Threshold Models Bias Correction

Develops a threshold spatial Durbin panel model in which spatial dependence and covariate effects switch jointly across regimes, with fixed-effect bias correction and bootstrap inference.

Read abstract

This paper proposes a spatial panel model in which the spatial-lag coefficient, the slopes on the covariates, and the slopes on their spatial lags switch jointly at an unknown threshold in an observed variable. Estimation proceeds by quasi-maximum likelihood (QML) after removing unit fixed effects, together with an analytical correction for the variance bias that this removal introduces. We establish the large-sample properties of the threshold and slope estimators and propose a bootstrap test for whether a threshold is present or not. Monte Carlo evidence across panel sizes typical of regional economics shows that the bias correction is necessary for accurate variance estimation and that the corrected estimator improves on both a single-regime spatial specification and a threshold specification without spatial dependence. Applied to sixty economies observed over four decades, the model illustrates its use by locating a development threshold near eight thousand five hundred dollars per capita in initial income, above which growth transmission across borders is markedly stronger than below it, a distinction that neither the single-regime nor the non-spatial threshold specification can express.

Common-Shock-Robust Inference for Extreme Panel Quantiles

Econometrics Extreme Quantiles Common Shocks

Proposes an inference framework for extrapolated extreme panel quantiles that accounts for synchronized common shocks with heterogeneous exposure across units, with an empirical illustration using food-inflation risk.

Read abstract

Extreme conditional quantiles in panel data are commonly obtained by extrapolating from intermediate quantile estimates, an approach that becomes fragile when many units are pushed into the tail by the same aggregate events. In such settings, pervasive common shocks do not only create cross-sectional dependence in the final statistic; they may also enter the intermediate anchor, distort the local spacing used for extrapolation, and concentrate the residual exceedances from which tail shape is learned. This paper develops an inference procedure for extrapolated extreme panel quantiles that is robust to synchronized shocks with heterogeneous exposure across units. The method defines a common-correlated-effects proxy target, estimates CCE-augmented fixed-effects quantile anchors, and derives a joint expansion that carries anchor, spacing, and tail-index uncertainty into the final extreme quantile through a time-cluster multiplier. The Monte Carlo evidence shows that, when common-shock exposure is heterogeneous, interacted CCE adjustments substantially improve coverage, reduce prediction error, and prevent residual tail events from being dominated by a small number of dates. An empirical illustration using monthly food-inflation acceleration in 51 developing economies shows that accounting for common shocks lowers measured aggregate q99 tail risk relative to unadjusted extrapolation, while preserving a clear development-vulnerability gradient across countries. The proposed framework therefore provides both an inferential correction and a diagnostic protocol for assessing whether extreme panel quantile conclusions are driven by genuine tail heterogeneity or by synchronized common-shock episodes.

Who Bears the Burden of Monetary Tightening? Credit Default Responses Across Income Groups and Firm Sizes in Brazil

with Ramon Lima Ribeiro and Victor L. Stilita

Macro-Finance Credit Default Heterogeneity

Studies heterogeneous monetary transmission to credit default in Brazil using disaggregated Central Bank data by income bracket, firm size, and state.

Read abstract

This paper examines heterogeneous monetary transmission to credit default in Brazil, exploiting granular disaggregation by income bracket, firm size, and state. Using monthly Central Bank data from 2012 to 2024 covering all 27 Brazilian states, we document three central patterns with direct policy relevance. We find robust Granger-predictive relationships from the benchmark interest rate to default in specific population and business segments, with responses typically becoming visible after roughly 6-7 months for individuals and 10-14 months for corporations; temporal horizons consistent with international literature on monetary policy transmission through credit channels. We document, for individuals, that the broader central segment of the income distribution, spanning 1-10 minimum wages, from lower-middle to middle-income households, displays the strongest responses relative to lower or higher income groups; for corporations, small firms display the clearest and strongest responses, while micro firms also react but with less precise aggregate evidence. Third, we show that predictive causality plus impulse responses can still deliver policy-relevant guidance without full structural identification. Additional mechanism exercises using occupational, sectoral, and product-level splits from the same credit registry suggest that the firm-side heterogeneity is most strongly associated with institutional credit segmentation, especially free and short-term credit.

From Climate-Vulnerability Indicators to Environmental Policy Targeting: Classification Rules and Public Resource Allocation in Northeast Brazil

with Fabrício C. Linhares

Climate Policy Targeting Public Resource Allocation

Examines how climate-vulnerability indicators are converted into priority groups and whether those groups align with public resource allocation channels in Northeast Brazil.

Read abstract

Administrative climate-vulnerability indicators are increasingly used to guide adaptation planning, drought response, and territorial targeting. Yet their policy value depends not only on measurement, but also on how continuous scores are converted into operational priority groups and linked to allocation procedures. This article examines this measurement-classification-allocation problem using Ceará's Municipal Alert Index, an annual climate-vulnerability indicator covering all 184 municipalities from 2004 to 2025. We compare the official threshold-based classification with two persistence-sensitive Rectangular Latent Markov alternatives, one using the twelve component indicators and another using the aggregate index. We then assess whether the resulting priority groups align with formula-based municipal transfers, subsidised regional development credit, and discretionary parliamentary allocations. The results show that classification design materially changes both the geography and temporal stability of climate-policy priority. The official rule produces substantial annual relabelling, whereas the Markov approaches identify more persistent territorial profiles. However, more credible classification does not automatically translate into vulnerability-responsive allocation: transfers, credit, and discretionary spending each follow distinct institutional logics. The findings suggest that climate-vulnerability indicators become effective environmental-policy instruments only when measurement, classification, and allocation are jointly designed.

Sectoral Productive Shocks and Poverty Reduction in Brazilian Microregions

Poverty Shift-Share Design Regional Development

Estimates the reduced-form relationship between microregional exposure to national sectoral wage-bill shocks and poverty reduction in Brazil between the 2000 and 2010 censuses.

Read abstract

We estimate the reduced-form effect of Brazilian microregions' exposure to national sectoral shocks on poverty from 2000 to 2010. Using a shift-share design based on predetermined sectoral composition, a one-standard- deviation increase in predicted wage-bill exposure reduces the poverty headcount by 0.48 percentage points, the poverty gap by 0.46 percentage points, and poverty severity by 0.40 percentage points. The estimates are stable across official microregions, alternative Bartik weights, state-clustered and shift-share-robust standard errors, and the exclusion of refined petroleum. Permutation inference is more conservative, and Rotemberg weights show that identification is concentrated in extractive and petroleum-related sectors. We therefore interpret the results as reduced-form evidence on the local poverty incidence of sectoral exposure, not as proof that broad formalization is the causal channel.

Nowcasting Brazilian Quarterly GDP: Mixed-Frequency Econometric and Machine Learning Models Under Multiple Performance Criteria

Forecasting MIDAS Machine Learning

Evaluates a real-time nowcasting framework for Brazilian quarterly GDP using mixed-frequency econometric models, machine-learning methods, and multiple forecast-performance criteria.

Read abstract

This paper evaluates a real-time nowcasting system for Brazilian quarterly GDP that combines a mixed-frequency data flow with alternative econometric and machine-learning approaches. Using a pseudo real-time design over 2015Q1-2025Q1 and four forecast horizons (nowcast through three quarters ahead), we benchmark ten model classes under three predictor environments: a full panel of monthly indicators and two reduced panels obtained via LASSO and One-Covariate-at-a-time Multiple Testing (OCMT) preselection. We assess performance along dimensions relevant for policy institutions: point accuracy (RMSE and MAE), directional and turning-point measures, tail-risk and low-growth detection, and multiple-model inference. The results show that model rankings are not invariant to the information environment or to the timing of the nowcast within the quarter. In the full panel, mixed-frequency shrinkage, sgLASSO-MIDAS, delivers the strongest average accuracy, while LASSO preselection shifts the frontier toward regularized linear prediction - Elastic Net. Under OCMT, mixed-frequency econometric models remain competitive and threshold MIDAS exhibits particularly strong crisis-relevant performance. Multiple-model inference indicates that several configurations are statistically hard to distinguish, supporting a short-list approach complemented by implementability and monitoring objectives. Overall, effective real-time GDP monitoring in Brazil benefits from matching model structure, variable selection, and the decision loss faced by institutions.

Intersectional Lower-Tail Wage Rigidity in Brazil: Evidence from Census Data and Synthetic Cohorts

ANPEC Regional Labor Economics Inequality Quantile Regression

Documents intersectional lower-tail wage disadvantage in Brazil using the 2010 Census, quantile methods, RIF-based decompositions, and PNAD Contínua synthetic cohorts.

Read abstract

This paper documents intersectional lower-tail wage rigidity in Brazil, defined strictly as a distributional pattern across groups rather than as institutional wage stickiness or individual dynamic entrapment. Using the 2010 Brazilian Demographic Census, we compare workers across race/color, gender, and Northeast residence and ask whether disadvantage appears not only in average wages, but also in bottom-tail representation, quantile-specific penalties, nonlinear wage gradients, and wage-structure components. The empirical strategy combines descriptive bottom-decile measures, conditional quantile regressions, RIF-based unconditional quantile regressions, threshold-style quantile models, and RIF decompositions, complemented by PNAD Contínua synthetic cohorts for 2012-2023. Northeast Black/Brown women and men are disproportionately represented in the bottom decile, face large lower-tail penalties, and display persistent cohort-level Q10 gaps across repeated cross-sections. The results provide a coherent set of distributional and cohort-level facts consistent with mechanisms emphasized in the persistent-poverty, inequality-of-opportunity, and regional-development literatures.
